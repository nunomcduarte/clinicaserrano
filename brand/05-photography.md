# Photography

Photography is the second surface mode of the brand (the first being the typographic paper-greige posts). It carries weight in the feed by being **warm, candid, and material-led** — never clinical-stock, never staged-corporate.

## Subject categories observed

1. **Team portraits** — doctors and clinical staff, posed but relaxed.
2. **Candid moments** — a doctor mid-listen with a patient (face partly out of frame), a staff member smiling between tasks.
3. **Interiors** — clinic rooms, the gym, treatment rooms, lit shelves.
4. **Equipment / objects** — close-ups of tools (dumbbells, plates) treated as still life.

## Light

The single most defining photographic choice:

- **Warm, low-key, available light.** Window light filtered through sheer curtains; LED strip lighting in interiors; tungsten-ish indoor warmth.
- **No on-camera flash.** No hard top-down medical fixture light. No clinical white-blue cast.
- **Soft shadows** — diffused, never crushed black. The mood is gentle, not dramatic.
- White-balance leans warm intentionally. A neutral or cool grade would break the brand.

## Composition

- **Vertical / portrait orientation** for Instagram (4:5 or taller).
- **Shallow depth of field.** Subject in focus, environment falls into a soft, warm blur. Equipment shots and interiors can use deeper DoF when the texture *is* the subject.
- **Negative space matters as much as in the typographic posts.** Don't fill every corner. Subjects are often offset, with quiet background occupying half the frame.
- **Eye contact is optional, not required.** Several portraits show subjects looking away mid-conversation — these read more authentic than "stare-into-the-lens" headshots.

## Color & texture in-frame

The recurring palette inside photographs aligns tightly with the typographic surface palette:

- Warm wood (oak, light walnut)
- Cream / off-white walls and fabric
- Pale ceramic
- Soft greenery (small potted plants)
- Black accents (faucet, gym flooring, weight plates) used sparingly for graphic punctuation
- The branded **navy** appears on textiles (towels, see `06-applications.md`)

When commissioning or reviewing new photography, ask: *would this frame look at home next to a paper-greige carousel slide?* If the colors fight, the photo doesn't belong in the system.

## Post-processing

- **Warm grade**, gentle contrast curve. Highlights softened, shadows lifted.
- **Grain is fine to keep** — it complements the paper texture of the typographic surfaces.
- **Skin tones natural** — never over-smoothed, never beauty-filtered. The brand reads honest.
- **No color tints** (no teal-and-orange, no faded vintage filters).
- **Crop carefully** — Instagram's 4:5 means portraits often need to lose hands or shoulders. Prioritize face and eye-level over showing the full body.

## When the wordmark appears in-frame

Across the photographs the wordmark shows up naturally on:

- **Lab coat embroidery** — small, dark thread on white, chest-pocket area.
- **Towels** — cream wordmark on navy textile, neatly folded.
- **T-shirts** — small charcoal wordmark printed centrally on the chest, just below the neckline.

These are styling choices, not graphic overlays. Treat them as part of the composition — frame them gently, don't shoot them like product packshots.

## What to avoid

- ❌ Stock photography of any kind.
- ❌ Smiling-doctor-with-clipboard clichés.
- ❌ HDR, heavy retouching, beauty filters.
- ❌ Direct flash, hard top light, cool fluorescent cast.
- ❌ Crowded compositions with multiple competing subjects.
- ❌ Group photos lined up against a wall ("staff photo day" energy).
- ❌ Text or graphics laid on top of photographs.
- ❌ Watermarks or social handles burned into the corner.

## Provisional

- Confirm whether there's a single photographer / consistent shooter (the lighting language is too coherent to be ad-hoc).
- Confirm whether photography is ever cropped to square (1:1) for grid feed considerations, or always native 4:5.
- Confirm whether the brand uses any video/Reels and whether the same photographic language carries over.
